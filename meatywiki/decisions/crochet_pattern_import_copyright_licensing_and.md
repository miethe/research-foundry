# Decision Record: Crochet Pattern Import: Copyright, Licensing, and Legality Risk Bundle

## Context

- Etsy prohibits unauthorized replicas or copies of items as well as the patterns or designs that enable their creation. [claim:clm_001]
- Etsy defines counterfeit or unauthorized goods as items imitating an authentic good, particularly by using a brand's name, logo, or protected design without the brand owner's consent. [claim:clm_002]
- Etsy requires sellers to follow all applicable laws for the items they list, placing the legal-compliance burden on the seller. [claim:clm_003]
- Etsy's prohibition on enabling artifacts extends to kits, instructions, patterns, and designs that enable the creation of prohibited items, confirming patterns themselves can be banned independently of the finished good. [claim:clm_004]
- Etsy routes copyright and intellectual-property infringement reports to a separate, dedicated Intellectual Property Policy rather than handling them under the Prohibited Items Policy itself. [claim:clm_005]
- This version of the Prohibited Items Policy is effective until August 11, 2026, on which date a successor policy with updates to Section 2 takes effect. [claim:clm_006]
- The general TDM exception requires Member States to permit reproductions and extractions of lawfully accessible works and subject matter for the purposes of text and data mining. [claim:clm_007]
- Copies made under the general TDM exception may be retained only for as long as necessary for text and data mining purposes. [claim:clm_008]
- The general TDM exception applies only where the rightholder has not expressly reserved the use in an appropriate manner, such as machine-readable means for content made publicly available online. [claim:clm_009]
- For content made publicly available online, rights reservation is appropriate only via machine-readable means including metadata and a website's or service's terms and conditions. [claim:clm_010]
- A separate mandatory TDM exception exists for research organisations and cultural heritage institutions with lawful access, and the general Article 4 exception does not affect it (it cannot be overridden by rights reservation). [claim:clm_011]
- Member States were required to transpose the directive into national law by 7 June 2021, and both TDM exceptions require lawful access as a precondition. [claim:clm_012]
- The sui generis right is a property right, similar to but distinct from copyright, that exists to recognize the substantial investment in obtaining, verifying, or presenting a database's contents. [claim:clm_013]
- The directive's sui generis arrangements let the database creator prohibit unauthorized extraction and/or reuse of the database's contents. [claim:clm_014]
- The right to prevent unauthorized extraction and/or reuse of a database's contents lasts 15 years from the date the creation of the database was completed. [claim:clm_015]
- Lawful users may extract and reuse non-substantial parts of a database without authorization, but may not carry out acts that unreasonably harm the legitimate interests of the database creator or content provider. [claim:clm_016]
- The directive applies to databases regardless of form (electronic/online or non-electronic/offline) and does not apply to the software used to make or operate the database or to the works and materials it contains. [claim:clm_017]
- Sui generis rights are economic rights and can be transferred, assigned, or granted under contractual licence. [claim:clm_018]
- The directive provides two distinct protections for databases: copyright for the intellectual creation in selecting and arranging materials, and sui generis protection for substantial investment in obtaining, verifying, or presenting contents. [claim:clm_019]
- Users posting patterns must represent and warrant that they hold the copyright and other necessary IP rights in every pattern they list. [claim:clm_020]
- Posting a pattern grants Ravelry only a non-exclusive, revocable license to list and advertise the pattern and its associated media, not ownership. [claim:clm_021]
- Users may not modify, distribute, or create derivative works based on any pattern they do not own without an explicit separate license from the owner. [claim:clm_022]
- Pattern sellers must grant buyers at minimum a non-commercial, perpetual license for personal use of the pattern. [claim:clm_023]
- Users are prohibited from collecting or storing personal data about other users except as explicitly provided for in applicable agreements or guidelines. [claim:clm_024]
- Copyright infringement complaints are routed to Ravelry's Copyright Agent at legal@ravelry.com under a DMCA-style notice process. [claim:clm_025]
- Etsy's Terms of Use contractually prohibit users from crawling, scraping, or spidering any page of the Services, or reverse engineering the source code, without Etsy's express permission. [claim:clm_026]
- The same anti-scraping clause directs anyone wanting programmatic access to use Etsy's separate API under the API Terms of Use rather than scraping pages. [claim:clm_027]
- A separate anti-disruption clause prohibits interfering with the Services, explicitly including sending excessive requests to Etsy's site or platform, making bulk automated downloading a distinct violation beyond scraping. [claim:clm_028]
- Etsy grants users only a limited, non-exclusive, non-transferable, and revocable license to use its Services, subject to the listed restrictions. [claim:clm_029]
- By posting their content, sellers grant Etsy (and only third parties as authorized by Etsy in its sole discretion) a non-exclusive, worldwide, royalty-free, irrevocable, sub-licensable, perpetual license to use, reproduce, distribute, and prepare derivative works of that content. [claim:clm_030]
- Disputes default to mandatory individual arbitration, but parties retain a carve-out to seek injunctive relief in court for infringement or misuse of intellectual property rights, preserving a court path for IP enforcement. [claim:clm_031]
- Copyright law expressly excludes protection for ideas, procedures, processes, systems, methods of operation, and concepts, so the functional stitch sequence or technique of a crochet pattern is unprotectable. [claim:clm_032]
- Even when the Office registers a literary, graphic, or artistic description of a system or method of operation, protection extends only to the original expression, not the underlying method or system. [claim:clm_033]
- A mere listing of ingredients or contents, or a simple set of directions, is uncopyrightable, establishing the recipe analogy for unprotectable functional instructions. [claim:clm_034]
- A recipe registration can cover the written description, explanation, photographs, and illustrations, but never the underlying process, the resulting dish, or the procedures/methods of operation. [claim:clm_035]
- A recipe that creatively explains or depicts how or why to perform a particular activity may be copyrightable, indicating that creative instructional expression can qualify for protection. [claim:clm_036]
- Copyright protection may be available for a sufficiently creative selection, coordination, or arrangement of specific content, but is limited to that specific content and not to arranging any content that way. [claim:clm_037]
- Blank forms designed for recording information and that do not themselves convey information are uncopyrightable, as are the ideas, systems, or functional layout behind a form. [claim:clm_038]
- The KnitWit spec self-identifies importing patterns from anywhere as the app's single biggest business risk. [claim:clm_039]
- The spec assumes pattern IP/licensing must be handled carefully because many patterns are not legally scrape-and-import friendly, motivating consent-gated ingestion. [claim:clm_040]
- The product requires a structured Crochet IR (units, pieces, rows, constraints, assembly) — i.e., parsing and storing third-party pattern text, the activity under legal scrutiny. [claim:clm_041]
- Pattern import competes with My Row Counter, which already imports PDFs/webpages/videos and integrates with Ravelry. [claim:clm_042]
- Ribblr is cited as the incumbent positioning interactive ePatterns as its innovation, the competitor KnitWit's import/playback must differentiate against. [claim:clm_043]
- The spec recommends an amigurumi-first scope as the wedge where guided assembly and shaping matter most. [claim:clm_044]
- An Etsy API app must not display listing content more than six hours older than Etsy's live data, and must not cache or store content longer than reasonably necessary to serve users. [claim:clm_045]
- Etsy grants only a limited, non-sublicensable, non-transferable, non-exclusive and revocable license, solely to develop applications that integrate with Etsy Services. [claim:clm_046]
- Developers must not support, encourage, or facilitate the unauthorized downloading, copying, reproduction, or use of Etsy Members' products, photos or designs, or any infringement of IP rights. [claim:clm_047]
- Apps must request no more than the minimum amount of data needed from the Etsy API to provide sellers with the intended application (data minimization). [claim:clm_048]
- Developers must not reverse engineer the API or access internal/legacy Etsy APIs or data feeds, and must not create extra API keys to circumvent rate limits. [claim:clm_049]
- Etsy Members, not Etsy, own the content they create; the API conveys no license to redistribute designers' content, so developers must secure rights independently. [claim:clm_050]
- Etsy prefers DMCA infringement reports be filed through its Etsy Reporting Portal (etsy.com/ipreporting), with email to legal@etsy.com offered as the fallback channel. [claim:clm_051]
- A valid Etsy DMCA notice must include six elements: an authorized signature, identification of the infringed work, identification and specific location (listing URL) of the infringing material, the IP owner's name and notifier contact info, a good-faith-belief statement, and an accuracy/authorization statement under penalty of perjury. [claim:clm_052]
- Etsy warns that misrepresenting that material is infringing may expose the filer to liability for damages, including costs and attorneys' fees. [claim:clm_053]
- Etsy states that fraudulent or abusive notices, or other misuse of its Intellectual Property Policy, may result in account termination or other legal consequences. [claim:clm_054]
- A counter notice (for US-based reports) requires the counter-notifier to consent to federal court jurisdiction and to accept service of process from the party who filed the original notification. [claim:clm_055]
- Etsy's designated agent is Etsy, Inc., Attn: Legal Department, 117 Adams Street, Brooklyn, NY 11201, USA; the page was last updated Jun 16, 2023. [claim:clm_056]
- The Compendium of U.S. Copyright Office Practices, Third Edition is the U.S. Copyright Office's administrative manual explaining its practices and statutory duties under title 17, serving both as a staff manual and a public guidebook. [claim:clm_057]
- The Third Edition is the current authoritative version of USCO practices: first released December 2014, revised in 2017, and updated again with the version made available January 28, 2021 (announced in this 2021 Federal Register notice). [claim:clm_058]
- Chapter 900 of the Compendium was updated to implement the Supreme Court's separability standard from Star Athletica v. Varsity Brands, 137 S. Ct. 1002 (2017), as the guidance for claims involving useful articles. [claim:clm_059]
- Copyright registration does not protect the overall shape or utilitarian aspects of a useful article; protection extends only to design features that can be conceptually separated from the useful article. [claim:clm_060]
- Copyright law defines a useful article as one having an intrinsic utilitarian function that is not merely to portray the article's appearance or convey information, and the Office assesses this from inherent, observable characteristics rather than subjective intent. [claim:clm_061]
- Under the Star Athletica two-step test, the extracted artistic feature must qualify as a nonuseful pictorial, graphic, or sculptural work on its own, and the Office applies only that separability test, not the alternate lower-court tests the Supreme Court declined to adopt. [claim:clm_062]
- The Etsy Open API v3 enforces application-based rate limits composed of Queries Per Day (QPD) and Queries Per Second (QPS), applied at the API key level for both public and private auth. [claim:clm_063]
- The QPD limit does not reset on a fixed midnight-to-midnight cycle; Etsy uses a progressive Sliding Window Rate Limiting Algorithm that calculates usage over the last rolling 24-hour period. [claim:clm_064]
- Under the sliding window, as the oldest bucket exits the 24-hour window the quota consumed by its requests is immediately freed and becomes available for reuse. [claim:clm_065]
- Every successful response exposes usage headers including x-limit-per-second and x-limit-per-day, with the documented example values of 150 QPS and 100000 QPD. [claim:clm_066]
- Rate limits are checked QPS-first then QPD, and exceeding either returns HTTP 429 with a retry-after header indicating seconds to wait before retrying. [claim:clm_067]
- Applications needing higher limits must request an upgrade by emailing developer@etsy.com with a detailed app description and an estimate of required QPD/QPS usage. [claim:clm_068]
- Etsy advises maximizing existing quota by implementing caching to reduce redundant API calls and by using exponential backoff retry strategy on 429 responses rather than immediate retries. [claim:clm_069]
- Etsy operates DMCA notice-and-takedown via the Etsy Reporting Portal and a Designated Agent under the DMCA, the easiest channel to report alleged infringement against shop listings. [claim:clm_070]
- Etsy disclaims making legal determinations of infringement and instead removes material cited for alleged infringement when given a policy-compliant report. [claim:clm_071]
- Etsy accepts counter notices for US-based copyright reports only; removed material may be restored 10 business days after a counter notice unless the owner files a court action or a qualifying Copyright Claims Board (CCB) action and informs Etsy. [claim:clm_072]
- Etsy terminates selling privileges of members subject to repeat or multiple infringement notices and reserves the right to refuse all services to terminated members who attempt to re-open a shop. [claim:clm_073]
- Etsy may share the reporting party's name and email with the affected member, and may reject reports or counter notices it believes are false, fraudulent, incomplete, or in bad faith. [claim:clm_074]
- The policy explicitly names the Copyright Claims Board (CCB) as a qualifying small-claims venue whose action can block restoration of removed material, confirming the CCB as a real enforcement avenue. [claim:clm_075]

## Decision

Designer opt-in licensing is the only model whose legal basis is affirmative consent rather than an exception or implied permission, making it the defensible default; the IR pattern.license and pattern.designer fields should be mandatory provenance metadata so every stored pattern carries a machine-readable consent record. [claim:clm_inf06]

## Rationale

- Sui generis and copyright rights can be licensed by the owner (clm_018); Ravelry warrants designer ownership (clm_020) and bars unlicensed derivatives (clm_022); the IR v0.1 already has pattern.license/designer fields, so capturing explicit consent as metadata operationalizes the only affirmative-consent path. [claim:clm_inf06]
- Scraping breaches three independent Etsy clauses (clm_026/028/047) and clm_027 explicitly redirects programmatic access to the API; since the API itself bars redistribution, the only clean mitigation is consent-gated ingestion rather than any scraping path. [claim:clm_inf13]
- Prose explanation, photos, and illustrations are protectable (clm_035/036/060) and the API bars copying photos/designs (clm_047); restricting persistence to ops+counts (the method, clm_037-adjacent) is the mitigation that keeps stored data on the unprotected side of the line. [claim:clm_inf14]
- clm_024 (no collecting others' personal data) and clm_048 (data minimization) jointly constrain what the importer may retain about a designer beyond the license/provenance record needed to evidence consent. [claim:clm_inf15]
- The spec names import-from-anywhere the #1 risk and prescribes opt-in/licensing/user-private mitigation (clm_039/040); the ToS evidence (clm_022/026/047/050) shows scraping and redistribution are barred while opt-in and personal-use licenses (clm_022/023) are permitted, yielding a three-class gate that matches the governance rules. [claim:clm_inf17]
- 17 USC idea/method exclusion (clm_032/033) plus the recipe analogy (clm_034-036) and Star Athletica useful-article separability (clm_060-062) jointly mean only original expression is protected; the stitch procedure and the finished useful article are not. [claim:clm_inf01]
- If the stitch sequence is an unprotectable method (clm_032-034) and protection is limited to the specific expression/arrangement (clm_037), then a structured IR (clm_041) capturing only ops and counts captures the idea, not the protected expression - though the creative selection/arrangement of rounds could still be partly mirrored, hence medium confidence. [claim:clm_inf02]
- Etsy ToU bars scraping and grants only a limited revocable license (clm_026/029); API ToU adds freshness/anti-redistribution limits (clm_045/047); Ravelry bars derivatives without license (clm_022). These are contract obligations decoupled from copyrightability, so feasibility-of-extraction does not equal permission. [claim:clm_inf03]
- Durable IR storage/redisplay conflicts directly with the six-hour freshness and no-persistent-cache rule (clm_045), the no-redistribution-license fact (clm_050), and the anti-facilitation clause (clm_047); data minimization (clm_048) further narrows permitted use. [claim:clm_inf04]
- Opt-in obtains the explicit owner license clm_022 demands; personal-copy rides clm_023's perpetual personal-use license but a 3D/IR transform may be a derivative barred by clm_022; marketplace redistribution requires rights the platforms do not convey (clm_047/050) and depends on aggregating many designer licenses. [claim:clm_inf05]
- clm_023 grants buyers a personal-use license while clm_022 forbids derivative works without a separate license; an IR/3D rendering arguably creates a new arrangement (clm_037 territory), so the two clauses point in opposite directions for the same act - an explicit contradiction with the personal-use scope undefined. [claim:clm_inf07]
- Both regimes leave the method/useful article unprotected (clm_032/060), but the EU sui generis right (clm_013-015) lets a database maker bar extraction of substantial parts and bars lawful-user acts that harm legitimate interests (clm_016) - a layer with no clean US analogue, creating jurisdictional divergence on bulk import. [claim:clm_inf08]
- Article 4 allows TDM reproductions (clm_007) only with lawful access (clm_012), only retained as needed (clm_008), and only absent a reserved right that website T&Cs can express in machine-readable form (clm_009/010); a persistent consumer-facing IR library exceeds 'as necessary for TDM' and platform ToS likely reserve the right. [claim:clm_inf09]
- The documented enforcement machinery is notice-and-takedown plus account termination (clm_025/070/073) and the CCB small-claims path (clm_072/075); these are the cheap, fast channels designers actually have, so enforcement most likely manifests through them rather than federal litigation. [claim:clm_inf10]
- The IP carve-out (clm_031) keeps courts available to rightholders, but counter-notice (clm_055), misrepresentation liability (clm_053), and CCB restoration-blocking (clm_072) give a licensed importer a documented defense channel, so takedowns are contestable not automatically fatal. [claim:clm_inf11]
- A competitor already imports from many sources (clm_042) and API throughput exists (clm_063/066), demonstrating technical feasibility; but the contractual ceiling (clm_026/045/047) is much lower, so a feasibility demo must never be read as legal clearance. [claim:clm_inf12]
- The enforcement evidence (clm_025/051/052/070/072/073/075) documents the takedown/CCB/termination machinery but no source card names a specific resolved designer-vs-importer dispute; honest G1 reporting must label the precedent base as mechanism-level, not outcome-level. [claim:clm_inf16]

## Consequences

- Scraping designer storefronts or platform pages is the single highest-severity legal exposure in the import ambition (critical severity, medium-high likelihood): it simultaneously violates Etsy's anti-scraping clause (clm_026), the anti-disruption/excessive-request clause (clm_028), and the anti-facilitation rule (clm_047), with the only documented-safe mitigation being to abandon scraping entirely in favor of designer opt-in upload or authenticated user-private import. [claim:clm_inf13]
- Storing the verbatim pattern text and designer photographs/illustrations in the IR is a high-severity exposure with a concrete mitigation: persist only the functional IR ops and expected_stitch_count (the unprotectable method) plus a provenance/license record, and never persist the designer's prose, photos, or original illustrations, which are the protectable expression (clm_035, clm_037, clm_060). [claim:clm_inf14]
- Data-protection exposure rides alongside the IP risk: Ravelry's clm_024 bars collecting other users' personal data and Etsy's API clm_048 mandates data minimization, so an import pipeline that ingests a designer's name/contact embedded in a pattern must store only the minimum provenance needed for the consent record and strip incidental personal data. [claim:clm_inf15]
- Recommended decision rule for KnitWit ingestion: GATE every import on a consent class - (A) designer opt-in upload with explicit IR license metadata = allow durable IR + 3D redisplay; (B) authenticated user-private import of a pattern the user lawfully holds = allow IR + 3D only in that user's private workspace, no sharing; (C) any scrape, unconsented API redistribution, or marketplace resale = block by default and require human legal review - mapping directly onto the spec's governance rule against unlicensed ingestion and no scraping of private marketplaces. [claim:clm_inf17]
- A written crochet pattern is legally bifurcated: the functional stitch sequence, technique, gauge, and resulting amigurumi item are unprotectable ideas/methods/useful articles, while the designer's original prose explanation, illustrations, photos, and creative selection-coordination-arrangement of the text are protectable expression. [claim:clm_inf01]
- Because copyright does not reach the functional stitch procedure, extracting a normalized Crochet IR (ops mr/ch/sc/inc/dec, expected_stitch_count, rounds) that discards the designer's prose and illustrations re-encodes only the unprotectable method, materially lowering copyright-infringement exposure relative to storing or redisplaying the verbatim pattern text. [claim:clm_inf02]
- Even a copyright-clean IR extraction is not consent-clean: Etsy's and Ravelry's contractual terms (anti-scraping, limited-license, six-hour freshness, no-derivative-works, data-minimization) create an independent breach-of-contract and ToS-violation exposure that survives whether or not the pattern text is copyrightable. [claim:clm_inf03]
- The Etsy API is a dead end for a pattern-import-and-redisplay product: clm_050 (Members own content, API conveys no redistribution license), clm_047 (must not facilitate unauthorized copying of designs), and clm_045 (no display older than six hours, no caching beyond necessity) together forbid the durable storage and re-display of a parsed pattern that KnitWit's IR requires. [claim:clm_inf04]
- Ranking the three consent models by residual legal risk: (1) designer opt-in licensing is lowest risk because it secures the express separate license that both Ravelry (clm_022) and copyright law require for derivative/redistribution use; (2) user-private personal-copy import is medium risk, leaning on the buyer's personal-use license (clm_023) but exposed to derivative-work and ToS limits; (3) licensed marketplace redistribution is highest residual risk and is out of scope per the spec governance rule against unlicensed/marketplace ingestion. [claim:clm_inf05]
- The user-private personal-copy model's defensibility hinges on a transform-vs-derivative question the evidence leaves unresolved: converting a lawfully purchased pattern into a Crochet IR and a 3D visualization for the buyer's own use may be a permitted personal use (clm_023) or a prohibited derivative work (clm_022), and that ambiguity is the model's central legal risk. [claim:clm_inf07]
- US and EU law converge on the import-product's core safe harbor (the functional stitch method/useful article is free to use under both the US idea-method exclusion and the limits of EU protection) but diverge on a distinct EU-only hazard: the Database Directive sui generis right can independently bar systematic extraction/reuse of a designer's pattern collection even where no individual pattern's text is copied. [claim:clm_inf08]
- The EU DSM Article 4 text-and-data-mining exception is not a reliable basis for KnitWit ingestion: it requires lawful access (clm_012), permits retention only as long as necessary for TDM (clm_008) rather than the durable IR storage the product needs, and is defeated by a rightholder's machine-readable opt-out which platform terms-and-conditions themselves can constitute (clm_010). [claim:clm_inf09]
- Real-world enforcement against an import app would arrive primarily as platform-mediated DMCA takedown and account termination, not litigation: Etsy and Ravelry both operate DMCA notice-and-takedown (clm_025, clm_070), Etsy terminates repeat infringers and refuses re-opened shops (clm_073), and the Copyright Claims Board is a documented low-cost small-claims venue (clm_072, clm_075) that lowers the barrier for individual designers to act. [claim:clm_inf10]
- The DMCA notice machinery cuts both ways for KnitWit: clm_031's IP carve-out from arbitration preserves a court injunction path for rights-holders, while clm_053/clm_054 (misrepresentation liability, bad-faith-notice account termination) mean KnitWit can defend opt-in/licensed content via counter-notice (clm_055) and CCB action (clm_072) rather than treating every takedown as conclusive. [claim:clm_inf11]
- Technical feasibility of import diverges sharply from legal permissibility: the My Row Counter precedent (clm_042) proves PDF/webpage/video import is a shipping capability and Etsy's API is rate-capable (clm_063, clm_066: ~150 QPS/100k QPD), yet clm_026 (anti-scraping), clm_045 (six-hour freshness/no-cache), and clm_047 (no facilitation of copying) make the legally permitted surface far narrower than the technically achievable one. [claim:clm_inf12]
- G1 (evidence-quality gate) is partially blocked for the IP workstream: the run satisfies the success criteria for US/EU statutory law and platform-ToS clauses with primary sources, but the 'at least 3 real-world enforcement/dispute examples' criterion is met only by documented platform mechanisms (Etsy DMCA process, CCB, repeat-infringer termination) and not by named adjudicated craft-community disputes, so the precedent base is procedural rather than case-specific. [claim:clm_inf16]
