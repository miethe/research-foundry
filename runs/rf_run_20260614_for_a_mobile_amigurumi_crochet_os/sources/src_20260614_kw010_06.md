# Source Card: Uncertainty Displays Using Quantile Dotplots or CDFs Improve Transit Decision-Making

## Summary
This ACM CHI 2018 paper (Fernandes, Walls, Munson, Hullman, Kay) ran a controlled, incentivized experiment in which people decided when to leave to catch a bus, comparing a no-uncertainty control, textual uncertainty, and uncertainty visualizations. It evaluated the quality of decisions rather than only probability extraction. Quantile dotplots (a discrete, frequency-framed encoding) produced the best decisions, CDFs performed nearly as well, and both beat textual uncertainty, establishing that how uncertainty is encoded -- not merely whether it is shown -- drives better-calibrated decisions.

## Key evidence
- (ev_001) Prior transit-uncertainty evaluations measured probability extraction, not decision quality; this study measures decision quality.
- (ev_002) Controlled, incentivized bus-timing experiment compared no-uncertainty control, textual uncertainty, and uncertainty visualizations.
- (ev_003) Quantile dotplots yielded better decisions than the alternatives.
- (ev_004) Quantile dotplots (50 outcomes) reached 97% of optimal expected payoff, 5 points above control.
- (ev_005) CDFs performed nearly as well; both outperformed textual uncertainty, which depended on the interval communicated.
- (ev_006) Quantile dotplots let users reason about probability as counts, not areas, fitting space-constrained mobile displays.
- (ev_007) Dotplot/CDF decision quality started high and improved with practice to ~95% of optimal by the final trial, growing more consistent.

## Limitations
- Domain is bus-arrival timing decisions, not a crochet/amigurumi UI; the paper discusses but does not test generalization to other domains.
- A foundational, stable 2018 design result cited as a confidence-signaling primitive, not a current-year finding.
