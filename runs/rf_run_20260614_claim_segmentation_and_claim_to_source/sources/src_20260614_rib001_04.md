# Source Card: Optimizing Decomposition for Optimal Claim Verification

## Summary
This ACL 2025 long paper (Lu, Ziems, Dang, Jiang; University of Notre Dame) studies the Decompose-Then-Verify paradigm for long-form factuality evaluation and shows that decomposition and verification interact: hand-crafted decomposition demonstrations misalign with downstream verifiers in "atomicity" (a novel information-density metric), producing suboptimal verification. The authors formalize optimal-decomposition-for-optimal-verification as a strongly NP-hard bilevel optimization problem and propose "dynamic decomposition," a reinforcement-learning framework that uses verifier feedback to decompose claims to verifier-preferred atomicity. It improves verification confidence by 0.07 and accuracy by 0.12 (0-1 scale) on average across verifiers, datasets, and input atomicities.

## Key evidence
- (ev_001) Introduces "atomicity" as a novel metric quantifying information density of a decomposed claim.
- (ev_002) Hand-crafted decomposition demonstrations misalign with downstream verifiers in atomicity, causing suboptimal verification.
- (ev_003) Optimal decomposition for optimal verification is framed as a strongly NP-hard bilevel optimization problem.
- (ev_004) "Dynamic decomposition" is an RL framework using verifier feedback to learn the decomposition policy.
- (ev_005) Dynamic decomposition improves verification confidence by 0.07 and accuracy by 0.12 (0-1 scale) on average.
- (ev_006) Official code is publicly released at github.com/yining610/dynamic-decomposition.
- (ev_007) Published at ACL 2025 (Long Papers), pp. 5095-5114, DOI 10.18653/v1/2025.acl-long.254.

## Limitations
- Reported gains (0.07 confidence, 0.12 accuracy) are averages "across varying verifiers, datasets, and atomicities" from the paper's own experiments; per-condition variance and external replication are not captured here. Scope is the Decompose-Then-Verify factuality paradigm, not claim verification generally.
