# Source Card: Can LLMs Evaluate Complex Attribution in QA? Automatic Benchmarking using Knowledge Graphs

## Summary
This ACL 2025 paper introduces CAQA, a 161,174-sample benchmark automatically generated from knowledge graphs to test whether LLMs can evaluate attribution (claim-to-source support) in QA. It defines a four-level complexity taxonomy (Single, Union, Intersection, Concatenation) and four fine-grained attribution categories (Supportive, Partially Supportive, Contradictory, Irrelevant). It finds that zero-shot evaluators including GPT-4 degrade sharply on multi-hop aggregated claims and on the Partially Supportive verdict, while fine-tuned detectors exceed 90% F1.

## Key evidence
- (ev_001) Four-level attribution complexity taxonomy; Union/Intersection/Concatenation are the multi-hop/aggregated cases.
- (ev_002) Four attribution categories beyond binary support, including an explicit Partially Supportive label for evidence missing some required facts.
- (ev_003) GPT-4 zero-shot F1 drops to 0.451 on Concatenation and 0.514 on Intersection.
- (ev_004) All evaluators underperform on Partially Supportive (highest zero-shot F1 only 0.456), the hardest verdict to detect automatically.
- (ev_005) Evaluators rely on keyword co-occurrence over semantic reasoning, misclassifying partially-supported claims as supportive.
- (ev_006) Benchmark has 161,174 KG-generated samples; fine-tuned detectors exceed 90% F1, indicating an evaluator-capability gap, not an unverifiable task.

## Limitations
- The granular per-category and per-complexity F1 numbers (e.g., 0.451, 0.514, 0.456) are reported in the paper's results tables/text; the verbatim quotes captured here are the descriptive sentences, while the precise figures are stated in the source's results section. Findings are specific to the KG-generated CAQA distribution and the evaluators tested (zero-shot/few-shot LLMs vs fine-tuned detectors).
