# Source Card: CiteME: Can Language Models Accurately Cite Scientific Claims?

## Summary
CiteME (Press et al., NeurIPS 2024) is the first manually curated citation-attribution benchmark in which each text excerpt unambiguously references a single specific paper, making the alignment unit claim-to-exact-source-document. It exposes a large gap between unaided frontier LMs (4.2-18.5%) and human experts (69.7%); the authors' GPT-4o-based CiteAgent, which can search and read papers, narrows this to 35.3%. The benchmark is small (130 samples) and adversarially filtered to remove instances GPT-4o could already answer from memory.

## Key evidence
- (ev_001) CiteME is the first manually curated benchmark whose excerpts each unambiguously reference a single paper (claim-to-single-source alignment unit).
- (ev_002) Unaided LMs reach only 4.2-18.5% vs 69.7% for humans; CiteAgent (GPT-4o, search+read) reaches 35.3%.
- (ev_003) Prior SOTA retrieval (SPECTER2/SPECTER) scores 0%; CiteAgent's best still trails humans by 34.4%.
- (ev_004) Adversarial filtering removed 124 GPT-4o-answerable samples, leaving 130.
- (ev_005) Human 69.7% was measured under a 2-minute cap (avg 38.2s), likely a lower bound on human skill.
- (ev_006) Curation by 4 ML grad students excluded "trivial" excerpts naming authors/acronyms to avoid testing mere memorization.
- (ev_007) A later evaluation reports GPT-o1 at 61.3% but with ~38.7% of the dataset estimated memorized (not verifiable in the v2 full text; quote omitted).

## Limitations
- Benchmark is small (130 samples) and confined to the machine-learning domain.
- Human 69.7% accuracy is capped by a 2-minute-per-item budget and likely understates true human ceiling.
- The GPT-o1 61.3% / ~38.7%-memorized figure (ev_007) could not be confirmed verbatim in the scraped arXiv v2 text; it is recorded from a later revision/secondary summary and carries no verbatim quote.
