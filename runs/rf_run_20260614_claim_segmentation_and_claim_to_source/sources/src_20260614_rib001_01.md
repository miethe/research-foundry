# Source Card: DnDScore: Decontextualization and Decomposition for Factuality Verification in Long-Form Text Generation

## Summary
This EMNLP 2025 paper (arXiv Dec 2024, Johns Hopkins) is the first to study the interaction between claim decomposition and decontextualization inside a single factuality-verification pipeline, rather than treating them independently. It identifies a core tension — decomposition isolates atomic facts while decontextualization re-inserts context, leaving it ambiguous which embedded fact to verify — and introduces DnDScore, a decontextualization-aware verification method that validates subclaims in the context of contextual information. Across configurations, ordering and context choices change factuality scores substantially (e.g., 33.00% FActScore baseline up to 61.51% DnDScore), with entity disambiguation (pronoun replacement) a leading cause of corrected verifications.

## Key evidence
- (ev_001) Decomposition and decontextualization have conflicting purposes, creating the central tension a verification system must resolve.
- (ev_002) Decontextualized subclaims are less atomic, making it unclear which embedded atomic fact to verify.
- (ev_003) FActScore (decompose-then-verify) baseline averages 33.00%.
- (ev_004) DnDScore peaks at 61.51% under Decontextualization-then-Decomposition with decontextualized claim as context.
- (ev_005) Decontextualization-then-Decomposition yields the most subclaims yet ~44.60% FActScore, near the reverse ordering.
- (ev_006) 48.52% of false-to-true judgment switches involved a pronoun replacement (entity disambiguation).

## Limitations
- Scores are method- and benchmark-relative (a biography factuality benchmark) and depend on the underlying verifier; absolute percentages are not directly comparable to other studies. Exact percentage-point "gain" framing in the originating brief is a derived comparison, not a single quoted figure — cite the underlying configuration scores (33.00 / 41.53 / 44.60 / 61.51) directly.
