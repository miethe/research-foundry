# Source Card: Compendium of U.S. Copyright Office Practices, Third Edition (Federal Register notice of update)

## Summary
This Federal Register notice (86 FR 3205, Docket No. 2019-03, published 2021-01-14) is the U.S. Copyright Office's official announcement of the latest update to its administrative manual, the Compendium of U.S. Copyright Office Practices, Third Edition, made available on the Office's website as of January 28, 2021. The Compendium is the authoritative manual of USCO examination practice and a public guidebook to its statutory duties under title 17. Of particular relevance, the notice explains how Chapter 900 was revised to implement the Supreme Court's Star Athletica separability standard for design features incorporated into useful articles, confirming that copyright protects conceptually separable design features but not the overall shape or utilitarian aspects of a useful article.

## Key evidence
- (ev_001) The Compendium is USCO's administrative manual explaining its practices and statutory duties under title 17, serving as both staff manual and public guidebook.
- (ev_002) The Third Edition is the current authoritative version (2014, revised 2017, updated 2021-01-28 per this notice).
- (ev_003) Chapter 900 was updated to implement the Star Athletica v. Varsity Brands (2017) separability standard for useful-article claims.
- (ev_004) Copyright does not protect the overall shape/utilitarian aspects of a useful article — only conceptually separable design features.
- (ev_005) A useful article is one with an intrinsic utilitarian function beyond portraying appearance or conveying information; assessed by observable characteristics, not subjective intent.
- (ev_006) Under the two-step test, the extracted feature must qualify as a nonuseful PGS work on its own; the Office applies only the Star Athletica separability test.

## Limitations
- This is the Federal Register notice announcing and explaining the update, not the full verbatim text of Chapter 900 itself (which resides as a separate document on copyright.gov); the notice paraphrases the two-step separability test.
- The notice does not, in this text, separately restate the 17 U.S.C. 102(b) exclusion of "methods, systems, or processes" as a quoted line; that exclusion is a broader Copyright Act principle inferable but not verbatim-quoted in this particular notice.
