# Source Card: Comparative Evaluation of Advanced Chunking for Retrieval-Augmented Generation in Large Language Models for Clinical Decision Support

## Summary
A controlled MDPI (Bioengineering) study built four otherwise-identical RAG pipelines differing only in chunking strategy — fixed-length recursive baseline, semantic clustering, proposition-based, and adaptive — and evaluated them on 30 postoperative rhinoplasty queries with a fixed retrieval stack. Advanced, meaning-aware chunking outperformed naive fixed-length splitting on retrieval precision/recall/F1 and clinician-graded accuracy and relevance, with adaptive chunking strongest. It establishes segmentation policy as a model-agnostic lever on RAG quality: aligning chunk boundaries to logical topic units, at modest preprocessing cost, improves what the retriever surfaces.

## Key evidence
- (ev_001) RAG quality depends on segmentation; fixed-length chunks split concepts and add noise, reducing precision.
- (ev_002) Naive equal-sized chunking dilutes meaning and surfaces incomplete or misleading information.
- (ev_003) Advanced chunking (semantic/proposition/adaptive) organizes text around meaningful units rather than token counts.
- (ev_004) Adaptive chunking beat the fixed baseline on retrieval metrics (P 0.50 / R 0.88 / F1 0.64 vs 0.17 / 0.40 / 0.24).
- (ev_005) Fixed recursive windowing performed worst — fragmenting coupled statements and duplicating content across overlaps.
- (ev_006) The adaptive strategy's extra preprocessing stayed practical by reusing precomputed embeddings and a single linear boundary pass.

## Limitations
- Narrow domain and small eval set (30 postoperative rhinoplasty queries); clinician ratings were subjective and inter-rater reliability was not reported; no formal ablation of similarity threshold, span length, or micro-headers. The paper does not use the terms "late chunking" or "contextual retrieval" — its advanced strategies are semantic, proposition-based, and adaptive (adaptive uses BART micro-headers, conceptually adjacent to contextual retrieval).
