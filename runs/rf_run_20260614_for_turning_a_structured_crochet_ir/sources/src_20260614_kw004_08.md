# Source Card: KnitWit B1 Workstream Report - Prior Art & Taxonomy

## Summary
This internal KnitWit research seed (a ChatGPT Deep Research synthesis) maps the prior art for crochet pattern visualization and generation. It defines the "Procedural Geometry via Pattern IR" family (compile a DSL into a stitch graph, then deterministically embed each stitch in 3D), records CrochetPARADE's licensing, export, and constraint-flagging behavior, and contrasts it with AmiGo's mesh-to-pattern inverse pipeline and Greer & Mould's force-directed simulation-lite layout. It frames the smallest forward prototype as Crochet IR -> stitch graph -> embed -> OBJ/GLTF with per-round indices for highlighting.

## Key evidence
- (ev_001) The Procedural Geometry via Pattern IR family parses a DSL/IR into a stitch graph and deterministically computes per-stitch 3D coordinates row by row.
- (ev_002) Retaining pattern structure guarantees fidelity to every inc/dec and makes row/stitch highlighting easy.
- (ev_003) CrochetPARADE flags overly loose or tight stitches to mitigate distortion when no physics is used.
- (ev_004) CrochetPARADE is GPLv3, computes client-side, has a public-domain grammar, and exports SVG charts and GLTF models.
- (ev_005) CrochetPARADE is not mobile-optimized; heavy patterns (thousands of stitches) can take minutes - the mobile-feasibility caveat.
- (ev_006) AmiGo segments the mesh, seeds a start point, builds a Crochet Graph, and emits instructions, placing inc/dec by curvature.
- (ev_007) Greer & Mould use a force-directed spring layout over a stitch graph as a simulation-lite alternative to full yarn physics.
- (ev_008) AmiGo assumes a smooth genus-0 mesh and notes curvature obstructions where sharp features defeat continuous stitching.
