# Source Card: BrowseComp: A Simple Yet Challenging Benchmark for Browsing Agents

## Summary
BrowseComp (OpenAI, April 2025) is a 1,266-question benchmark of hard, multi-hop web-browsing problems with short, single-string answers graded by exact-match-style AI comparison against a reference. It scores agents on two axes — answer accuracy and confidence calibration — with OpenAI Deep Research solving ~51.5% versus near-zero for non-reasoning models. It is explicitly scoped to short-answer retrieval and does not measure long-form report coherence or citation quality.

## Key evidence
- (ev_001) 1,266 hard multi-hop problems with short, easily verifiable answers; dataset and grader at openai/simple-evals.
- (ev_002) Grading is exact-match-style semantic-equivalence judging using the Humanity's Last Exam grader prompt.
- (ev_003) OpenAI Deep Research scored 51.5% accuracy; the paper reports Calibration Error (%) as a second axis for all models.
- (ev_004) GPT-4o with browsing rose only 0.6% to 1.9%; tool access without strategic reasoning barely helps (o1 without browsing hit 9.9%).
- (ev_005) Browsing-capable models show higher calibration error, so raw tool access can inflate confidence in wrong answers.
- (ev_006) Accuracy scales smoothly with test-time compute; 64-sample voting (majority/weighted/best-of-N) adds 15-25%.
- (ev_007) Benchmark measures only short-answer retrieval, NOT long-form response ergonomics or ambiguity resolution.

## Limitations
- Self-reported, first-party results: the headline 51.5% comes from OpenAI's own Deep Research model, which per footnote 1 is trained on data specifically teaching it to be good at BrowseComp tasks.
- Scope limit: by design it measures short verifiable answers only, so it cannot stand in for rubric-based scoring of report coherence or citation quality and must be paired with such measures.
