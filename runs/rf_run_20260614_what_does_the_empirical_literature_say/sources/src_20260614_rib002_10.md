# Source Card: Chain-of-Verification Reduces Hallucination in Large Language Models

## Summary
This paper introduces Chain-of-Verification (CoVe), a prompting/inference method in which a language model drafts an initial response, plans verification questions, answers those questions independently to avoid bias, and then produces a final verified response. The authors report that CoVe reduces hallucinations across list-based Wikidata questions, closed-book MultiSpanQA, and longform text generation. The work was submitted to arXiv in September 2023 and published in Findings of the Association for Computational Linguistics: ACL 2024.

## Key evidence
- (ev_001) CoVe is a four-step method: draft, plan verification questions, answer independently, generate final verified response.
- (ev_002) Independent answering of verification questions is the bias-avoidance mechanism that curbs repeated hallucinations.
- (ev_003) CoVe decreases hallucinations across Wikidata list questions, closed-book MultiSpanQA, and longform text generation.
- (ev_004) Hallucination is framed as plausible-but-incorrect factual information and an unsolved LLM problem.
- (ev_005) The method's premise is that LLMs can deliberate on their own responses to correct mistakes.
- (ev_006) Authored by Dhuliawala et al.; arXiv Sept 2023; published in Findings of ACL 2024 (pp. 3563-3578).

## Limitations
- The abstract reports directional results ("decreases hallucinations") without exposing the per-task quantitative magnitudes; specific accuracy/precision figures require the full paper body, not captured here. The intrinsic compute/latency overhead of running multiple inference passes (draft + N verification answers + final) is a known trade-off noted in source key points but not stated verbatim in the abstract.
