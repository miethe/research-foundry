# Source Card: Detecting and Correcting Reference Hallucinations in Commercial LLMs and Deep Research Agents

## Summary
This arXiv preprint (Rao, Wong, Callison-Burch; UPenn; April 2026) empirically measures reference hallucination across commercial LLMs and deep research agents on the DRBench evaluation set, finding 3-13% of cited URLs are fabricated and 5-18% are non-resolving overall. It introduces urlhealth, an open-source HTTP-HEAD-plus-Wayback verification tool, and shows that agentic self-correction reduces non-resolving citations 6-79x (p<1e-35) to under 1% confirmed-broken. The authors warn that support-only metrics systematically overestimate report reliability when fabricated URLs go undetected.

## Key evidence
- (ev_001) 3-13% of cited URLs are hallucinated and 5-18% are non-resolving across evaluated commercial LLMs and deep research agents.
- (ev_002) Per-model fabricated-citation rates on DRBench: gemini-2.5-pro-deepresearch 13.3%, gpt-4.1 5.4%, openai-deepresearch 3.5%, claude-3-5-sonnet-with-search 3.0%.
- (ev_003) Deep research agents cite far more URLs per query, yet volume alone does not determine reliability — retrieval architecture matters more than citation volume.
- (ev_004) urlhealth classifies each URL as LIVE/DEAD/LIKELY_HALLUCINATED/UNKNOWN via HTTP HEAD plus Wayback Machine snapshot lookups.
- (ev_005) Agentic self-correction reduced non-resolving citations 6.4-79x (p<1e-35), reaching under 1% across three models.
- (ev_006) With 3-13% fabricated URLs, support-only metrics systematically overestimate report reliability.

## Limitations
- v1 preprint, not yet peer-reviewed; rates are measured on the DRBench evaluation set and a specific model snapshot list, so they may not generalize to all query distributions or later model versions.
