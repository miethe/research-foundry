# Source Card: Enabling Large Language Models to Generate Text with Citations (ALCE benchmark)

## Summary
ALCE (Gao, Yen, Yu, Chen; Princeton NLP, EMNLP 2023) is the first benchmark for automatic evaluation of citations in LLM-generated text. End-to-end systems must retrieve supporting evidence and emit answers with inline citations, which are then scored on three automatic dimensions—fluency, correctness, and citation quality—that the authors show correlate strongly with human judgement. Citation quality is computed at the statement level using citation recall and citation precision, with an NLI entailment model (TRUE / T5-11B) judging whether the concatenated cited passages support each statement; even the strongest baselines leave roughly half of ELI5 generations not fully supported.

## Key evidence
- (ev_001) ALCE is the first benchmark for automatic LLM citation evaluation, requiring end-to-end retrieve-then-cite systems.
- (ev_002) Three automatic evaluation dimensions—fluency, correctness, citation quality—correlate strongly with human judgement.
- (ev_003) Built on three datasets: ASQA, QAMPARI, and ELI5, spanning different question types and corpora.
- (ev_004) Citation quality = citation recall (output fully supported by citations) + citation precision (no irrelevant citations).
- (ev_005) An NLI model (TRUE, a T5-11B fine-tuned on NLI data) checks whether cited passages entail the generated statement.
- (ev_006) On ELI5, even the best models (ChatGPT, GPT-4 baselines) leave ~50% of generations not fully supported by citations.
- (ev_007) Statement-level citation recall is 1 only if a citation exists and the NLI model judges the concatenated passages entail the statement.

## Limitations
- Benchmark, metrics, and reported citation-quality numbers reflect the 2023 model/retriever landscape (ChatGPT and GPT-4 era) and are dataset- and system-specific; absolute support rates may differ for later models.
