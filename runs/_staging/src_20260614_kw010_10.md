# Source Card: Understanding the Effects of Miscalibrated AI Confidence on User Trust, Reliance, and Decision Efficacy

## Summary
This controlled user-study paper measures how miscalibrated AI confidence (overconfident vs underconfident vs well-calibrated) shifts user trust, reliance, and decision accuracy. It finds miscalibration significantly increases over-/under-reliance and erodes the accuracy benefit of AI assistance, that confidence levels directly move switching behavior, and that merely disclosing miscalibration improves detection but does not improve decision efficacy (it trades over-reliance for under-reliance). The core design takeaway: confidence must be well-calibrated to help, and users need to understand WHY/when confidence may be unreliable.

## Key evidence
- (ev_001) Overconfident AI raised over-reliance to ~41.3% vs ~28.2% for well-calibrated output (significant).
- (ev_002) Underconfident AI raised under-reliance to ~17.7% vs ~11.2% for well-calibrated output (significant).
- (ev_003) Miscalibration shrank accuracy gains: +6.5% (under) and +7.2% (over) vs +11.9% (well-calibrated).
- (ev_004) Switch-to-AI rate fell to ~40.5% (under) and rose to ~69.6% (over) vs ~57.0% (well-calibrated).
- (ev_005) Disclosing calibration raised correct overconfidence detection from 26.2% to 73.8%.
- (ev_006) Disclosure yielded no efficacy gain because it increased under-reliance on overconfident AI.
- (ev_007) Design recommendation: tell users why/under what conditions confidence may be unreliable, not just a bare number.
- (ev_008) Experiment 1 N=126 (42 per condition).

## Limitations
- Findings come from generic crowdsourced decision tasks, not crochet/amigurumi UX; transfer to KnitWit confidence badges is an inference.
- Experiment 1 uses modest per-condition cell sizes (42 per condition); effect sizes for some contrasts are small.
