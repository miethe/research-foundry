# Source Card: Trust Development and Repair in AI-Assisted Decision-Making during Complementary Expertise

## Summary
This peer-reviewed ACM FAccT 2024 paper reports a between-subjects experiment (N=300, 150 per task) where participants classified familiar and unfamiliar stimuli alongside a simulated AI of varying accuracy. It shows that users transfer perceived AI accuracy on familiar, high-expertise tasks to calibrate trust on unfamiliar tasks they cannot verify, and that explicit trust-repair strategies differ sharply in effectiveness even under identical AI accuracy. Model Update was the strongest repair (restoring trust above its initial level), followed by Apology, Promise, and the no-repair baseline, with Denial being least effective and actively backfiring.

## Key evidence
- (ev_001) Survey-based between-subjects experiment, N=300 (150 per task), across geometric-shape and animal-identification classification with a simulated, varying-accuracy AI.
- (ev_002) Repair effectiveness order after trust erosion: Model Update (above initial) > Apology > Promise > no-repair baseline > Denial (least effective).
- (ev_003) Users employed perceived AI accuracy on familiar (HHE) stimuli as a heuristic to calibrate trust on unfamiliar (LHE) stimuli.
- (ev_004) Without performance indicators, users over-rely on the AI regardless of actual accuracy, even when explanations are provided.
- (ev_005) Early errors disproportionately shape whole-interaction trust; first impressions are costlier than later mistakes.
- (ev_006) Trust defined per Lee and See as an attitude that an agent will achieve a goal under uncertainty and vulnerability.
- (ev_007) Denial was least effective, backfiring to distrust despite later accuracy gains.
- (ev_008) Model Update (technical) outperformed Promise (behavioural) under identical accuracy; Promise only matched the baseline.

## Limitations
- Simulated AI rather than a real deployed model; survey-based crowd study with two specific classification domains (shapes, animals), so generalization to richer real-world Human-AI collaboration is unverified. AI made its sole Phase 1/3 mistake on a fixed trial (number 7), a controlled design choice rather than naturalistic error timing.
