# Source Card: Understanding the Effects of Miscalibrated AI Confidence on User Trust, Reliance, and Decision Efficacy

## Summary
This empirical HCI paper (arXiv 2402.07632) reports two controlled experiments measuring how miscalibrated AI confidence affects human trust, reliance, and decision quality. Against a fixed 70%-accurate AI, overconfidence (80% stated) drove over-reliance and higher switch-to-AI rates while underconfidence (60% stated) drove under-reliance; both miscalibration directions cut decision-accuracy gains roughly in half versus a well-calibrated 70% baseline. Users largely could not detect miscalibration themselves, and explicitly disclosing the calibration level improved detection but eroded trust without improving overall decision efficacy.

## Key evidence
- (ev_001) Three conditions against a fixed 70% AI accuracy: underconfident 60%, well-calibrated 70%, overconfident 80% stated confidence.
- (ev_002) Overconfidence raised switch-to-AI to ~69.6% vs ~57% well-calibrated; underconfidence lowered it to ~40.5%.
- (ev_003) Overconfidence raised over-reliance to ~41.3% vs ~28.2% well-calibrated.
- (ev_004) Underconfidence raised under-reliance to ~17.7% vs ~11.2% well-calibrated.
- (ev_005) Decision-accuracy improvement fell to ~6.5% (under) and ~7.2% (over) vs ~11.9% well-calibrated.
- (ev_006) 66.7% wrongly judged underconfident AI well-calibrated; 64.3% made the same error for overconfident AI.
- (ev_007) Communicating calibration level raised correct detection to 76.2% but reduced trust and did not improve decision efficacy.

## Limitations
- Controlled lab task with a synthetic AI fixed at 70% accuracy; results may not transfer directly to deployed, variable-accuracy assistants. Confidence/accuracy conditions are stylized (60/70/80% vs 70%). Preprint (arXiv), not a venue-final reference at time of carding.
