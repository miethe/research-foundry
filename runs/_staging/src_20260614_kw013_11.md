# Source Card: Legal protection: databases (Directive 96/9/EC) | EUR-Lex

## Summary
This official EUR-Lex legislative summary of EU Directive 96/9/EC establishes two parallel protections for databases: copyright for the intellectual creation in selecting/arranging materials, and a distinct "sui generis" property right protecting substantial investment in obtaining, verifying, or presenting a database's contents. The sui generis right lets a database maker prohibit unauthorized extraction and/or reuse of the whole or a substantial part of the contents, lasts 15 years from completion of the database, and applies regardless of the database's form (electronic or non-electronic). The directive has applied since 16 April 1996 (transposition deadline 31 December 1997); this summary was last updated 11 January 2019.

## Key evidence
- (ev_001) The sui generis right is a property right distinct from copyright, recognizing substantial investment in obtaining, verifying, or presenting database contents.
- (ev_002) Sui generis arrangements let the database creator prohibit unauthorized extraction and/or reuse of its contents.
- (ev_003) The right to prevent unauthorized extraction/reuse lasts 15 years from completion of the database's creation.
- (ev_004) Lawful users may extract/reuse non-substantial parts without authorization but may not unreasonably harm the creator's legitimate interests.
- (ev_005) The directive applies to databases irrespective of form (electronic/online or non-electronic/offline) and is separate from the contained works/software.
- (ev_006) Sui generis rights are economic rights that can be transferred, assigned, or licensed.
- (ev_007) Two distinct protections coexist: copyright for selection/arrangement, and sui generis for substantial investment.

## Limitations
- This is the official EUR-Lex summary, not the binding directive text; for legally precise wording (e.g., "substantial part," exact exceptions) consult the directive itself (OJ L 77, 27.3.1996, pp. 20-28). The sui generis right is an EU-specific construct and the summary notes protection is granted to EU-national/EU-resident makers; non-EU jurisdictions may not recognize an equivalent right.
