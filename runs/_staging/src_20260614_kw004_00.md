# Source Card: Modeling crochet patterns with a force-directed graph layout

## Summary
Greer & Mould (Carleton University, Expressive + WICED 2025) present a tool that converts a written row-by-stitch amigurumi pattern into a stitch graph and computes a 3D model via the Isenburg et al. "Connectivity Shapes" force-directed layout, treating stuffing as graph inflation. The layout is a non-linear least-squares minimization of an edge-length energy plus a Laplacian curvature energy (solved with Ceres Solver), producing shapes "similar, though not identical" to real crocheted objects. The work establishes both the feasibility and the concrete performance/accuracy limits of graph-layout-based crochet modeling.

## Key evidence
- (ev_001) A written amigurumi pattern is translated into a stitch graph and laid out via the Isenburg [IGG01] Connectivity Shapes method, with stuffing modeled as graph inflation.
- (ev_002) Output models reproduce shape and size but are explicitly "similar, though not identical" to the real objects.
- (ev_003) Solved as least-squares of edge-length energy + Laplacian curvature energy via (1-lambda)*E_L + lambda*C with static lambda=0.65 (0.9 for bunny head/body), using Ceres Solver.
- (ev_004) Hard convergence ceiling by node count: legs (318) and arms (264) converged under 5s; head (798) and body (708) did not converge after well over two minutes.
- (ev_005) Higher-curvature apple took 30s+, versus a few seconds for small patterns.
- (ev_006) Convergence is often unnecessary; iterating past a point made no noticeable difference, motivating a raised threshold and a quality/run-time trade-off for users.
- (ev_007) Real-time workflow is incremental/local: run a few solver iterations on only the changed region after each edit for near-instant feedback.
- (ev_008) Curvature is minimized as a proxy for the least-squares-incompatible goal of maximizing volume, yielding sub-maximal volume.
- (ev_009) Tested only on beginner stuffed single-crochet patterns; flat/2D rows unsupported; two metrics may be insufficient and a one-size-fits-all layout is unlikely.

## Limitations
- Scope: validated only on a small set of beginner-level, stuffed, single-crochet-like amigurumi objects; flat/2D row pieces and intermediate/advanced patterns were explicitly excluded.
- Performance figures ("under 5 seconds", "well over two minutes", "30 seconds or more") are reported qualitatively for specific example objects, not as systematic benchmarks; exact hardware is not captured in this card.
- An upfront global solve on a pre-written pattern carries an "unavoidable" run-time cost; the near-instant feedback claim applies to the incremental edit workflow, not the cold global solve.
