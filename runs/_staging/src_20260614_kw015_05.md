# Source Card: Modeling Crochet Patterns with a Force-directed Graph Layout

## Summary
This peer-reviewed Eurographics Expressive/WICED 2025 paper (Greer and Mould, Carleton University) presents a structural-geometry method that converts a written amigurumi crochet pattern into a graph (stitches as nodes, physical connections as edges) and computes a force-directed graph layout to produce a 3D preview. The resulting model matches hand-crocheted pieces in shape and size -- though not identically -- letting designers iterate on a digital model before physically crocheting. It targets both professional designers and beginners; performance and accuracy are bounded by uniform edge-length assumptions and slow convergence on large patterns.

## Key evidence
- (ev_001) Written pattern is translated into a graph and a force-directed graph layout produces a 3D representation.
- (ev_002) Resulting 3D model matches the hand-crocheted piece in shape and size, enabling preview before physical crocheting.
- (ev_003) Audience is professional designers and beginners; oriented to amigurumi but extensible to clothing/similar styles.
- (ev_004) Each stitch is a node; physical connections between stitches are edges (sequential, working, constraint).
- (ev_005) Layout is a non-linear least-squares optimization over edge-length and curvature energies (Isenburg et al. [IGG01]), solved with Ceres Solver, lambda = 0.65 for most pieces.
- (ev_006) Performance degrades with size: bunny head (798 stitches) and body (708 stitches) did not converge after over two minutes.
- (ev_007) Models were similar but not identical to real pieces; some error from the uniform edge-length-of-1 assumption.
- (ev_008) Least-squares minimization is incompatible with maximizing volume, so the method minimizes curvature, yielding sub-maximal volume.

## Limitations
- Tested only on simple beginner-level amigurumi patterns and single-crochet-like stitches; flattened (2D) pieces excluded. Models structural geometry only, not yarn physics, texture, or fuzziness. Force-directed layout is slow and may not converge on large patterns. Piece-to-piece attachment was specified ad hoc. The similar web app "Crochet Parade" is undocumented, limiting comparison.
