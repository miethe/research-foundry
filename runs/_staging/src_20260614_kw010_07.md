# Source Card: "Are You Really Sure?" Understanding the Effects of Human Self-Confidence Calibration in AI-Assisted Decision Making

## Summary
This peer-reviewed ACM CHI 2024 paper investigates calibrating a human's own self-confidence as a lever for achieving appropriate reliance on AI in AI-assisted decision making. Across three studies, it shows that calibrating human self-confidence improves human-AI team performance and encourages more rational reliance compared to an uncalibrated baseline, while reducing under-reliance but not over-reliance. It treats trust (a subjective attitude toward the system) and reliance (the behavior of adhering to or overriding a recommendation) as separable constructs, and defines inappropriate reliance as over-reliance and under-reliance, giving measurable targets for a confidence-aware UI.

## Key evidence
- (ev_001) Frames appropriate reliance as a human-centered problem addressed via "human self-confidence calibration" across three studies.
- (ev_002) Calibrating self-confidence enhanced human-AI team performance and produced more rational reliance vs an uncalibrated baseline.
- (ev_003) Trust = subjective, self-reported perception of AI; reliance = objective behavior toward AI — separable constructs to design for.
- (ev_004) Over-reliance = agreeing with AI when it is wrong; under-reliance = rejecting AI when it is right — measurable UI targets.
- (ev_005) Study 3: calibration yielded more rational reliance, reduced under-reliance and improved task performance, but did not reduce over-reliance.
- (ev_006) Self-reported trust does not reliably predict reliance behavior, so reliance behavior is the more dependable appropriateness signal.

## Limitations
- Findings come from a single task domain (income prediction on the Adult Income dataset) with non-expert crowd participants in a lab-style user study; generalization to other domains and to a crochet/amigurumi assistant context is not established. Over-reliance was not reduced by the intervention.
