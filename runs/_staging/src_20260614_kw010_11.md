# Source Card: Critical or Compliant? The Double-Edged Sword of Reasoning in Chain-of-Thought Explanations

## Summary
This arXiv preprint reports an empirical user study on how Chain-of-Thought (CoT) explanations from vision language models affect human trust and error detection in multimodal moral scenarios. By systematically perturbing reasoning chains and varying delivery tone, the authors find that surfacing step-by-step reasoning is double-edged: it can clarify, but it also drives confirmation bias and overreliance. Two effects stand out — users equate trust with outcome agreement (relying even on flawed reasoning), and a confident tone suppresses error detection while sustaining reliance, so delivery style can override correctness.

## Key evidence
- (ev_001) Explanations meant to provide transparency can instead foster confirmation bias when outputs look acceptable.
- (ev_002) The study perturbs reasoning chains and manipulates delivery tone for VLMs in multimodal moral scenarios.
- (ev_003) Users equate trust with outcome agreement and keep relying even when reasoning is flawed.
- (ev_004) A confident tone suppresses error detection while maintaining reliance — delivery style can override correctness.
- (ev_005) CoT explanations can simultaneously clarify and mislead; systems should encourage scrutiny over blind trust.

## Limitations
- Evidence is from VLM moral-scenario tasks, not crochet/pattern UX; applying it to KnitWit's inc/dec-placement rationale overlays is analogical inference.
