# Source Card: Evaluating the Impact of Uncertainty Visualization on Model Reliance

## Summary
A peer-reviewed IEEE TVCG (2023) controlled experiment by Arizona State University researchers (Zhao, Wang, Mancenido, Chiou, Maciejewski) testing how uncertainty visualization affects human reliance on a model in a college-admissions forecasting task on Amazon Mechanical Turk. Using a within-subjects design across five visual representations (Table, Histogram, Model-prediction, Violin Plot, Question-mark) and two task-difficulty levels, it finds that reliance depends jointly on task difficulty and machine uncertainty, and that ordinal forms of expressing uncertainty better calibrate model-usage behavior. It frames the core invariant that miscalibrated trust drives over- or under-reliance, and that the cognitive accessibility of the visualization is decisive.

## Key evidence
- (ev_001) Reliance depends jointly on task difficulty and machine uncertainty, not uncertainty alone; ordinal forms better calibrate usage.
- (ev_002) Study design: two uncertainty visualizations, college-admissions forecasting, two difficulty levels, Amazon Mechanical Turk.
- (ev_003) Failure to calibrate trust causes over- or under-reliance, both of which diminish the joint human-automation system.
- (ev_004) Without intervention, people are biased to adopt imperfect human judgments over imperfect models.
- (ev_005) Five representations compared across three categories: Table/Histogram, Model-prediction, Violin Plot/Question-mark.
- (ev_006) A simple ordinal (Question-mark) representation is more cognitively accessible to general audiences than continuous probability distributions.

## Limitations
- Single forecasting domain (college admissions) with an MTurk crowdworker sample rather than domain experts; the experimental model's "low uncertainty == reliable" mapping is, by the authors' own note, not necessarily true for all models. Author's accepted version (content may change before final publication).
