# Source Card: Benchmarking Multi-Agent LLM Architectures for Financial Document Processing

## Summary
This March 2026 arXiv preprint by Siddhant and Yukta Kulkarni systematically benchmarks four multi-agent orchestration patterns (sequential, parallel fan-out, hierarchical supervisor-worker, and reflexive self-correcting) across five frontier and open-weight LLMs on 10,000 SEC filings. It reports concrete cost-accuracy tradeoffs: reflexive loops achieve the best field-level F1 (0.943) at 2.3x baseline cost, while hierarchical orchestration sits on the favorable Pareto frontier (F1 0.921 at 1.4x cost), and hybrid configurations recover 89% of reflexive accuracy gains at just 1.15x cost. It is an on-topic primary source for tiered-pipeline economics in extraction-heavy workloads.

## Key evidence
- (ev_001) Four orchestration architectures benchmarked: sequential, parallel fan-out, hierarchical supervisor-worker, reflexive self-correcting.
- (ev_002) Corpus of 10,000 SEC filings (10-K/10-Q/8-K) across five frontier and open-weight LLMs.
- (ev_003) 25 extraction field types measured along five axes including cost per document and token efficiency.
- (ev_004) Reflexive F1 0.943 at 2.3x cost; hierarchical F1 0.921 at 1.4x cost (Pareto-favorable).
- (ev_005) Hybrid configs recover 89% of reflexive accuracy gains at 1.15x baseline cost.
- (ev_006) Scaling from 1K to 100K docs/day shows non-obvious throughput-accuracy degradation curves.
- (ev_007) Positioned as actionable empirical guidance for regulated financial deployments.

## Limitations
- Numbers are verified from the abstract only; the compressed PDF body and Tables 1-12 (cost-per-document dollar figures, latency in seconds, specific model names) could not be reliably extracted, so per-architecture detail beyond the abstract headline figures is unconfirmed here.
- Peer-unreviewed arXiv preprint; results are single-corpus (SEC filings), so generalization to other document classes is not established.
