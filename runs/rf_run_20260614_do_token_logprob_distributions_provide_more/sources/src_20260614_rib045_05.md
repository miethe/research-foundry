# Source Card: Are LLM Decisions Faithful to Verbal Confidence?

## Summary
This arXiv preprint (USC, Jan 2026) introduces RiskEval, a framework that tests whether frontier LLMs adjust their abstention policies as error penalties vary. It documents a dissociation: models can verbalize calibrated uncertainty yet do not act on it — they keep answering even when high penalties make abstention mathematically optimal, producing "utility collapse." The core takeaway is that calibrated self-reported confidence is not a sufficient basis for trustworthy, risk-sensitive AI decisions.

## Key evidence
- (ev_001) Verbal confidence is neither cost-aware nor strategically responsive under high-penalty conditions.
- (ev_002) Invariance to risk: raising the incorrect-answer penalty across [0.1, 100] has a negligible effect on confidence or the answer/abstain decision.
- (ev_003) Models almost never abstain even when abstention is mathematically optimal, causing utility collapse.
- (ev_004) Confidence and action are decoupled: models "know" their uncertainty but fail to convert it into a good abstention policy.
- (ev_005) Calibration is poor and dataset-dependent — on HLE, ECE 0.474-0.841, Brier 0.332-0.791, AUARC 0.041-0.533.

## Limitations
- arXiv v1 preprint (not yet peer-reviewed); metric ranges differ across datasets (HLE/GPQA/GSM8K) and models, so the headline "poorly calibrated" finding is strongest on hard benchmarks (HLE) and weaker on easy ones (GSM8K). No single sentence in the paper states a cross-all-models headline range.
