# Source Card: Quantifying and Mitigating Self-Preference Bias of LLM Judges

## Summary
This arXiv preprint (2604.22891v2, submitted 2026-04-28) defines self-preference bias (SPB) as a directional deviation in which an LLM judge systematically favors or disfavors its own outputs, measured as beta_i = rho_i - rho_i^null. Across 20 mainstream models evaluated on 100 AlpacaEval tasks, SPB ranged from +0.307 (LongCat-Flash-Chat) to -0.229 (Claude-Sonnet-4.5), and model capability was not protective against bias. It proposes a mitigation that decomposes holistic judgment into pairwise forced-choice decisions over five dimensions (Relevance, Accuracy, Depth, Logic, Clarity), cutting average SPB by 31.5% (up to 69.9% for the worst case).

## Key evidence
- (ev_001) SPB is defined as a directional evaluative deviation favoring/disfavoring own outputs, quantified as beta_i = rho_i - rho_i^null.
- (ev_002) SPB ranged from +0.307 (LongCat-Flash-Chat) to -0.229 (Claude-Sonnet-4.5) across 20 models on 100 AlpacaEval tasks.
- (ev_003) Advanced model capability is uncorrelated, or even negatively correlated, with low SPB.
- (ev_004) Mitigation uses a pairwise forced-choice protocol over five dimensions: Relevance, Accuracy, Depth, Logic, Clarity.
- (ev_005) The mitigation reduced average SPB by 31.5%, up to 69.9% for the worst-biased model.
- (ev_006) Human validation showed 79.8% agreement on deliberately selected hard boundary cases.

## Limitations
- arXiv preprint, not yet peer-reviewed. Findings rest on a 100-question AlpacaEval slice and 20 models as of April 2026; the metric (rho_i, rho_null) and PIR construction are paper-specific and should be cited with their definitions. Specific model rankings reflect the April 2026 model snapshot.
