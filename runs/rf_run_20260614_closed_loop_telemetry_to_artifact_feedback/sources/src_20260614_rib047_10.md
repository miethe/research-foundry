# Source Card: Choosing a Sequential Testing Framework - Comparisons and Discussions

## Summary
A Spotify Engineering post by experimentation data scientists Marten Schultzberg and Sebastian Ankargren that compares sequential testing frameworks (Group Sequential Tests, mSPRT, GAVI/Always Valid Inference, and Bonferroni-corrected z-tests) for online experimentation. It frames "peeking" as a false-positive inflation problem and argues that framework choice hinges on two parameters: batch-vs-streaming data delivery and whether maximum sample size is predictable. A simulation study reports relative power across methods, generally favoring GST when sample size is known and AVI when it is not.

## Key evidence
- (ev_001) An intended 5% false-positive rate climbs toward ~10% after just two interim analyses (the peeking problem).
- (ev_002) Two parameters drive framework choice: batch vs streaming data, and whether max sample size is estimable.
- (ev_003) GST is in most cases the highest-power method, even when expected sample size is overestimated.
- (ev_004) A 50x sample-size underestimate costs AVI ~15% power vs correctly tuned GAVI and ~30% vs correct GST.
- (ev_005) With few analyses, Bonferroni-corrected z-tests are as effective as always-valid approaches.
- (ev_006) AVI needs no advance sample size and supports streaming, but is weaker on batch than on streaming data.
- (ev_007) GST requires a known/estimable max sample size and is not feasible to run in streaming mode.

## Limitations
- Power and false-positive figures are author-reported results from Spotify's own simulation study under specific assumptions (e.g., 0.2 SD effects, chosen n and rho values across 14-56 analyses) and were not independently replicated; treat exact percentages as illustrative of the simulated regime rather than universal constants.
