# Source Card: How we built our multi-agent research system

## Summary
This Anthropic engineering blog post (published June 13, 2025) describes the production architecture of Anthropic's multi-agent Research feature. It documents an orchestrator-worker design in which a Claude Opus 4 lead agent decomposes a query, spawns parallel Claude Sonnet 4 subagents with their own context windows, synthesizes their findings, and hands off to a dedicated CitationAgent. It reports self-measured performance and token-economics findings: a 90.2% gain over single-agent Opus 4 on an internal eval, token usage explaining ~80% of BrowseComp variance, and multi-agent systems consuming ~15x the tokens of chat.

## Key evidence
- (ev_001) Research system uses an orchestrator-worker pattern: a lead agent coordinates and delegates to parallel specialized subagents.
- (ev_002) Opus 4 lead + Sonnet 4 subagents outperformed single-agent Opus 4 by 90.2% on Anthropic's internal research eval.
- (ev_003) Token usage alone explained 80% of performance variance on BrowseComp (three factors explained 95% total).
- (ev_004) Agents use ~4x more tokens than chat; multi-agent systems use ~15x more tokens than chats.
- (ev_005) The LeadResearcher saves its plan to Memory because exceeding the 200,000-token context window causes truncation.
- (ev_006) A dedicated CitationAgent processes documents and the report to identify specific citation locations after research completes.
- (ev_007) Lead spins up 3-5 parallel subagents and each subagent uses 3+ tools in parallel, cutting research time by up to 90% on complex queries.

## Limitations
- Performance figures (90.2% improvement; 80% variance share) are self-reported by Anthropic on internal evals and a BrowseComp analysis, not independently replicated. Architecture reflects the system as of June 2025 and may have since changed.
