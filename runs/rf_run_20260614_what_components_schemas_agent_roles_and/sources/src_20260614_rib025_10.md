# Source Card: Enabling Large Language Models to Generate Text with Citations

## Summary
This EMNLP 2023 paper introduces ALCE, the first benchmark for Automatic LLMs' Citation Evaluation. ALCE requires end-to-end retrieve-then-cite systems and scores outputs on three dimensions — fluency, correctness, and citation quality — using automatic metrics shown to correlate strongly with human judgement. Citation quality is decomposed into citation recall (is the output fully supported by its cited passages) and citation precision (are any cited passages irrelevant); experiments find that even the best models lack complete citation support 50% of the time on ELI5.

## Key evidence
- (ev_001) ALCE is the first benchmark for automatic LLM citation evaluation, requiring end-to-end retrieve-then-cite systems.
- (ev_002) Metrics span three dimensions (fluency, correctness, citation quality) and correlate strongly with human judgement.
- (ev_003) Citation quality = citation recall (full support by cited passages) + citation precision (no irrelevant citations).
- (ev_004) On ELI5, even the best models lack complete citation support 50% of the time.
- (ev_005) Three datasets (ASQA, QAMPARI, ELI5) span question types and corpora from Wikipedia (21M passages) to web-scale.
- (ev_006) ALCE is positioned as reproducible, unlike prior work reliant on commercial search engines and human evaluation.

## Limitations
- Published 2023-05 (EMNLP 2023); the "best models" benchmarked predate mid-2026 frontier models, so the 50%-citation-support figure reflects 2023-era systems and may have improved since. Dataset-specific corpus/answer-type details (e.g., ELI5 uses the Sphere corpus) are summarized from Table 1 context and the paper body, not all individually quoted.
